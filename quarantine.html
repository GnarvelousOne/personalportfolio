ARE YOU QUARANTINED? You probably are. Here are some ways I have been passing the time:


    Web

  • Zooniverse - This project crowdsources ordinary people all over the world to help scientists sift through massively large data sets. I have been helping identify errors in the Laser Interferometer Gravitational-Wave Observatory (LIGO) project operated by Caltech and MIT. From the website:

    "The LIGO data is full of ‘glitches’, signals from non-astrophysical sources like small ground motions near the observatories. Both glitches and true gravitational wave signals appear as specific patterns in the images. We can correctly identify gravitational wave signals only if we are able to reliably distinguish them from glitches; like finding a needle in a haystack. The research team cannot identify all the glitches on their own because: 1) There are just too many images. With millions of images and only a few researchers working on glitch identification and removal, it would take them decades to do this without your help. 2) Computers lack the ability to reliably identify patterns - the human eye is still much better at this type of task."

    There's tons of different projects covering a range of topics. But you will be teaching machines how to learn. So you're making a Skynet!

  • Tetris - The greatest game ever! My high score is 180,000. If you're going for the gold, here's a few tips. Try to only complete lines with a "Tetris", that is, a 4-line combo. You will go up in level and difficulty as you complete lines. You get more points for one Tetris than you would for 4 single lines, or even two 2-line combos. You'll have to "burn off" some lesser combos but that's fine my dude, just keep your eye on those 4-bangers. You will score more points per level. Save the long bars until you are ready for that rad Tetris.

    Check out this CTCW Tetris Quarterfinal from 2019 - it is probably the greatest official match that has ever taken place: Joseph vs. Green Tea
